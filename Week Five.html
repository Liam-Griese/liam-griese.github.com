Week Five: Shaper

Liam Griese's Introduction to Digital Fabrication Website

Introduction

The Shaper is a handheld wood cutter that is moved by hand to cut wood. It uses cameras on the front of it to build a virtual enviornment for itself and projects a picture of what it is cutting onto a screen so that the user can see it. It has a bit that spins very fast and slowly cuts out wood at a certain height. The machine also has a different bit that is tapered so that it can engrave words onto wood. The initials on my design were made by this and that is why they look different.

Learning to use the Shaper

The first thing you have to do for tha shaper is get your design onto the usb that goes in the shaper and get yourself a piece of wood. The shaper is only used on a specific sized piece of wood, which means you have very little choice in that department

The second step is to attact the wood to the table. This is done with a few strips of double sided tape, as well as a screw in two of the corners, as shown above. Once that is done, you have to take a new scan with the shaper itself. It uses the camera to orient itself and build the virtual version of the table. Once this is done, you can place your design into the virtual world, making sure that it all is within the limits of the piece of wood you are cutting.

Once the wood is secure, and our design is uploaded into the virtual world, you can move on to cutting part. But before you put shaper to wood, you have to make sure you turn the vacuum on and make sure you have ear and eye protection.

Once your safety gear is on, alert everyone to the fact that you are about to begin cutting, and then you can begin.

I learned that you should begin with all of the inside cuts, and save the big outline cut for last. This is becasue that once the outline is cut out, the piece of wood will have less support and stability. When you go around for the second time, you leave little nubs connecting the project to the scrap wood. Then, you can go back around with a hammer and chisel and break them. This ensures that the design is stable the whole time you are using the machine.

Designing with Fusion 360 and Illustrator

The first thing I did was play around with making shapes. I followed the tutorial that was in the shaper week assignment and it helped me a good deal. I documented the process here. I just made a simple rectangle with a circle inside of it.

Once I had done a fair amount of just messing aorund in the program, I began to work on the actual project. I statred with a fresh file. The first thing I made was a rectangle that was the size of the wood we were going to use: 12.5 in x 8.5 in. This would work as an outline for the rest of the design process. Next I made a smaller rectangle inside of the larger one, this would be the actual piece I was going to cut out from the rest. I curved the edges so that it would look better. Much like your video, I started by making the circle with the smaller circle within it. This was one of the requirements of the project. Then I stared to build the shapes that would eventually be the holes that would fit my stuff. I went with my wallet, my pencils, my thumb drive, and my airpods case. I brought all of the items to the lab and measured them. I started with the usb, and made a rectangle that would be the same size as it, I added a few more for good measure, in case on day I had a few thumb drives. I used lines to make sure they were equal distance apart from each other. Next I moved on to the large wallet hole. Then to the pencil holes and the airpod holes. Originally I made a few pencil holes, but later took out all but one becasue I feared it would be a lot of work to do many. Once I had all the holes I began to make the design 3d by selecting various pieces and adding height with 'e.' By making the frame and the piece itself highest, and the holes lower, it created the design I wanted. I then rendered it and added a wood texture. Then, with a little bit of trouble, I exported the file as a dxf, converted it to a .ai file, and then uploaded it into Illustrator. From there I copied the color chart, posted it into the file and began to color the different parts according to what type of cut I wanted them to be.
